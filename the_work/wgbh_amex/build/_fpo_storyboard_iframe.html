  3. +

    Memories

    Date Submitted: October 24, 2009

    I was born in l936 the second child to my parents. My father was a motorman for the Chicago Rapid Trnsit Authority in Chicago for 30 years or more and raised 4 children on $35. a week. My mother never worked outside the home. The city was a wonderful place to grow up in. My parents bought several properties in Chicago while I was growing up, always looking for a better place for us. I never remember any of us being labeled to any financial class. My father and mother always had food and shelter for us and we didn't know we weren't rich. Once a year we would go to Riverview when my father took vacation. Nobody had money those days so there was noone to envy nor was there TV to tell us what to buy. All of us siblings started to work at 10 or 12 learning the value of saving for Christmas presents, buying our own clothes and working for a higher education. We were happy and didn't rely on welfare if things were bad. I don't remember my parents ever saying so. They never discussed finances with us.

    I remember wanting a pair of ice skates one Christmas, I must have been 10 or so. I pleaded with my parents to buy them for me. My father would look in the want ads of the Chicago Tribune newspaper hoping to find used toys for us, i.e. electric train for my brother and ice skates for myself. Of course, Christmas morning we had our wish gifts under the tree. My parents were thrifty and expected us to contribute to the family household which we did. Twenty dollars a week went a long way.

    Our social life revolved around relatives who lived close by. We always got together during the holidays and there would be more than 20 at a gathering. My father worked nights almost all his life and to have him present at Thanksgiving and Christmas was the best gift ever.

    Good Memories,
    Judy Lane

    My Grampa, Monster of Folk

    Date Submitted: October 14, 2009

    It seemed rather improbable for a person who spent his formative teenage years and early twenties driving an asphalt truck to learn so many instruments, or know by heart almost every Woody Guthrie song my kids could find on wikipedia. But somehow that was my Grampa, a person who seemed just magically able to pick up any instrument and play.

    After perhaps my third try at learning to play the guitar I asked my Grandfather how he had learned to play all the instruments he could (I think it was seven: Piano, Guitar, Banjo, Ukulele, Harmonica, upright Bass and Organ). He told me that when he was first trying to get work in the early thirties, he worked day to day as a helper on coal and oil trucks that would ply between Boston, New Hampshire and Vermont. To pass the time waiting for work he would bring a harmonica out and a Ukulele and play for all the boys waiting.

    When one of the drivers saw this, and because there were no radios in the trucks, he picked my grandfather as his helper, despite Grampa being, he would recall later, "a scrawny little urchin who probably wasn't much help." From there he said he learned all kinds of instruments to entertain the drivers while on the road, and when he became a driver himself, to pass the night when they had to stop off in the upper reaches of New England in the woods.

    That story about the Thirties always stayed with me.

    Gifts from the 30s

    Date Submitted: October 9, 2009

    Both of my grandparents grew up and raised a family in the midst of the depression, but they were always very giving and generous people.

    The weirdest thing was, until the day they died, whenever they got presents they would carefully un-stick the tape from the wrapping paper, while remarking "isn't this such nice paper," and unfold the paper then fold it back up placing it somewhere for safe keeping. Only then did they turn their attention to the gift.

    It was something small, but it was something that always stuck with me when seeing my kids rip open their Christmas or Birthday presents.
